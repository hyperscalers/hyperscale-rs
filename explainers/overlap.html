HYPERSCALE · FORK SAFETY

Why the chain cannot fork.

A committee where up to a third are corrupt. Two competing petitions. And one stubborn arithmetic fact: any two supermajorities must share an honest member. The deepest safety property in the system rests on a proof you can check on your fingers — used twice.

01 · The stakes

The cardinal sin

A fork is two versions of history, both claiming to be final: block #100 says Ada's coin went to Bo, the other block #100 says it went to Cy, and different parts of the world believe different copies. Every promise a blockchain makes collapses at that moment — "final" meant nothing, and the same coin was spent twice.

For Hyperscale the stakes compound, because everything else in the system leans on each shard having exactly one history: cross-shard transfers prove state against a shard's committed blocks (the generals), the beacon records each shard's boundary blocks as fact (the clock), and a dying shard's will is read from its one true chain. A forked shard wouldn't just double-spend locally — it would poison every proof drawn from it. So everything narrows to the one property under all the others: within a committee where fewer than a third are corrupt, two conflicting blocks can never both be committed.

02 · The arithmetic

One village, two petitions

Picture a village council of ten electors, of whom at most three are corrupt — you don't know which. Nothing becomes official without a petition signed by seven. Now suppose two competing petitions both claim to have passed in the same session. Count: 7 + 7 = 14 signatures, but only 10 electors exist — so at least 4 people signed both. At most 3 of those can be corrupt. Therefore at least one double-signer is honest.

And an honest elector follows one iron rule: one signature per session, ever. So the fourth double-signer is a contradiction — the second petition can never actually gather its seven. Not because anyone watched for cheating, but because the numbers don't leave room for it.

That's the whole engine. In Hyperscale each shard's committee plays the village, a block certificate is a petition (2f+1 signatures out of 3f+1 seats), and the honest one-signature rule is enforced by every validator on itself: at most one vote per round, ever. Two conflicting certificates in the same round are arithmetically impossible.

03 · The twist

Two certificates can exist. Two commits cannot.

Here's the part that surprises people who expect the obvious defense to be the whole defense. Rounds don't always succeed — a proposer stalls, the round times out, a new round begins (timeouts also burn rounds). And across different rounds, two sibling blocks at the same height genuinely can each gather a full certificate, without a single honest validator breaking any rule. The system does not prevent this — and doesn't need to.

Because a certificate is not a commitment. A block commits only when a certificate forms for a child at exactly the next round — an immediate, back-to-back confirmation. And the act of signing that confirmation carries a promise: every signer locks, vowing never again to endorse anything that doesn't build on what they just confirmed. Now the village arithmetic strikes a second time: any future certificate needs 2f+1 signatures, and any 2f+1 must include at least one honest elector from the confirming quorum — a promise-keeper whose lock forbids endorsing the rival branch. The rival may hold a certificate; it can never gather another one on top. It withers.

The design's honest shape: disagreement is allowed to exist; it is only forbidden to matter. Certificates are cheap and can conflict. Commitment is expensive precisely so that the arithmetic can guard it.

04 · The promises

Three rules every honest validator keeps

All of the above compiles down to a tiny personal discipline — two counters each validator maintains against itself, checked before every signature:

◦ ONE VOTE PER ROUND

No second thoughts

Vote or time out — either way the round is spent, forever. A round you gave up on can never be voted afterward, even if the missing block shows up late. (Signing twice anyway is self-proving treason: permanent expulsion — the audit.)

◦ THE LOCK ONLY RISES

Promises don't expire

Once locked on a block, a validator will only ever endorse things that build at least that high. There is no unlock rule — no input, no timeout, no leader's plea can lower it.

◦ NEVER VOTE BLIND

Hold the whole block first

A vote is cast only while holding the block's complete contents — which quietly turns every certificate into a custody receipt too (the archive).

And the promises survive the promiser: both counters are written to disk before any signature leaves the machine, so a validator that crashes and restarts wakes up still bound by every vow it ever made. A promise that could be forgotten by pulling a power cord would be no promise at all.

05 · The hunt

Trying very hard to break it

An argument this load-bearing doesn't get to rest on being persuasive. A dedicated adversarial harness runs shard consensus under hostile scheduling — messages delayed, dropped, reordered, delivered at the cruelest moments a seeded adversary can find — and after every run asserts one thing: no committable fork exists anywhere in the wreckage. Partitioned committees are watched for the correct behavior too: a committee split below quorum halts rather than diverging, and resumes cleanly on heal — safety never depends on timing, only liveness does. Every one of those runs replays byte-for-byte from its seed (the crash lab), so a counterexample, if one ever appeared, would be a permanent exhibit rather than a rumor. The restart case gets its own rehearsal: a crash-restart scenario — its script produced not by a seed but by a model checker holding every possible schedule at once (the proof) — kills a validator mid-promise and asserts the revived node still keeps it.

The lesson: don't build a system where disagreement is impossible — build one where disagreement can't be ratified. Let petitions conflict; make enactment require an immediate confirmation whose signers' promises, by pigeonhole, must surface in every future quorum.

One height, at most one committed block — not by vigilance, but by counting.
