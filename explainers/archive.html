HYPERSCALE · DATA AVAILABILITY

Published means preserved.

In 1610, an Oxford librarian struck a deal that quietly changed what "publishing" means: a copy of every new book must be lodged in his library — an obligation attached at the moment of publication, not a favor asked afterward. Hyperscale treats a blockchain's data the same way: nothing becomes history until enough machines provably hold every byte of it.

01 · 1610

Sir Thomas Bodley's deal

Thomas Bodley, rebuilding Oxford's ruined university library, understood something ahead of his time: a civilization's memory cannot depend on publishers' goodwill or collectors' luck. In 1610 he persuaded the Stationers' Company — the guild that controlled English printing — to lodge a copy of every book they registered with his library, as a standing condition of the trade itself. Parliament made the principle law in 1662, and "legal deposit" spread across the world; today six libraries in Britain and Ireland alone are entitled to a copy of everything published.

The genius is in the timing. Preservation isn't something that happens to a book later, if someone remembers, if the publisher cooperates, if a librarian notices a gap. The copy exists because the book does. Publication and deposit are one act — so nobody ever has to discover a loss and go begging for a reprint from a publisher who may be bankrupt, hostile, or gone.

The principle that outlived the printing press: attach the obligation to preserve at the moment of publication — never rely on detecting a loss and repairing it after the fact.

02 · The same problem, 400 years on

A ledger whose evidence is scattered

A sharded blockchain scatters its records by design. Shard Q's correctness routinely depends on data published by shard P: the transactions it must run, the provisions proving P's state (the generals), the certificates sealing P's results. And the delivery layer is gossip over a real network — lossy, reordered, unfair. The deadly subtlety is that absence is invisible: a node that never received something usually doesn't know there was a something. "I never got it" and "it never existed" look identical from the inside. The naive postures all fail:

✕ HOPE THE GOSSIP WORKED

You can't fetch what you can't name

Best-effort broadcast delivers almost everything, almost always — and the missing fraction is precisely the data nobody knows to ask for.

✕ KEEP EVERYTHING FOREVER

Not an architecture

"Never delete" turns every disk into a countdown clock, and still doesn't say who must serve what to whom — hoarding is not availability.

✕ ASK THE PUBLISHER NICELY

One node is not a guarantee

The machine that sent the data may have restarted, rotated off the committee, or be lying. A polite request to a single peer is hope with an address.

Hyperscale's answer runs on one design principle, applied everywhere: the artifact you need is either held by someone obligated to serve it, or provably expired. There is no third state — no shrug, no "ask around." That principle runs on three mechanisms: deposit, obligations, and provable forgetting.

03 · Deposit at publication

Every certificate is a deposit receipt

The masterstroke is hidden inside consensus itself. A validator votes for a block only while holding the complete block — the header and every transaction, wave, and provision body it references. Not a summary, not a hash list: every byte. Which means a quorum certificate does double duty (the overlap covers its first job): the moment 2f+1 signatures assemble, it is proven that 2f+1 machines each hold a full copy of everything the block carries — recoverable from any one of them.

Compare the ordinary alternative: certify a compact header now, distribute the heavy contents later, and discover during a crisis that "later" never came for the one block you need. Here the Bodley move closes that door structurally — a block cannot become official faster than it becomes preserved, because the signatures and the copies are the same act.

04 · The obligations ledger

Who must hold what, until when

Beyond blocks, the cross-shard machinery generates artifacts with specific consumers — and each travels with an explicit obligation: a named class of holders, and a release condition. Three rules give the ledger its teeth:

Release on receipt, not on silence. A shard that publishes provisions must keep serving them until the destination's own quorum-signed certificate covers the batch — a positive, cryptographic acknowledgment — or the attested deadline passes. "It's probably fine by now" is not a release condition anywhere in the system.

Absence is announced. Since a missing thing can't announce itself, block headers pre-announce: which shards this block provisions, and a fingerprint of what each should expect. A destination therefore knows there is something to miss — and after a grace period, fetches it from the obligated committee rather than waiting on gossip's mood. The same pattern covers transactions a destination learns about but never received.

Obligations survive restarts. Requests are answerable from committed storage — including historical reads within the retention window — so a source node that crashed and recovered can still honor its shard's obligations. The duty attaches to the role, not to a process's uptime.

05 · Provable forgetting

The archive that knows when to say no

The mirror half of preservation is disciplined forgetting — an archive that keeps everything forever eventually keeps nothing well. Every eviction in the system keys on BFT-attested time, which makes "we are allowed to forget this now" a consensus fact: the same on every honest machine, at the same attested moment. Past the horizon, a request isn't met with a shrug or a maybe — it is refused, identically, everywhere, and every protocol that consumes history is designed to complete its business within the window. A request can be satisfied or provably expired; it can never fall into the crack between.

Bodley's principle, restated for machines: an archive is not a building. It is a set of obligations attached at the moment of publication — held until receipted, or released by a clock everyone shares.

And the loss paths are rehearsed, not hoped away: the fault suite kills each gossip channel in turn and asserts the fetch obligations recover it — the crash lab.
