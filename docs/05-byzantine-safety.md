# Byzantine Safety

This document collects the system's defenses against adversarial validators and adversarial networks: the threat model and the committee security model behind it (why sampled committees stay honest, what failure means, what recovers and what prevents it), the sampling and shuffling machinery, jailing, the cryptographic layer, what one shard trusts about another, and the network-level hardening. Fork safety within a shard is treated in [01-consensus-layers.md](01-consensus-layers.md) §1; §1 below covers why its committee-level assumption is reasonable, and the rest of the document the machinery around it.

Main code homes: committee sampling/shuffling, the randomness fold, halt recovery, and validator lifecycle in `crates/beacon` (fold modules), quorum math and signing types in `crates/types`, equivocation tracking and Byzantine input bounds in `crates/shard`, message classing in `crates/types` network module, fault injection in `crates/network` and `crates/scenarios`. The statistical analysis behind §1 lives in `specs/committee_security.{md,py}` — the quantitative source of truth, whose measurement tier drives the shipped fold code as a CI regression net (`crates/beacon/tests/committee_security.rs`); this document carries the conclusions in plain language.

---

## 1. Threat model

**Per committee, classical BFT.** Every consensus decision — shard QC, execution certificate, beacon proposal certificate — is made by a committee of size n assuming at most f Byzantine members with n ≥ 3f+1. A beacon block additionally commits only through a ratification quorum of the whole serving pool, so a committee draw that lands fully Byzantine can certify content but never commit it ([01 §3.1](01-consensus-layers.md)). Quorums are strict >2/3 (`VoteCount::has_quorum`); voting power is per seat (one validator, one vote), with stake acting as an admission gate rather than as vote weight — a dynamic, demand-tracking minimum stake governs who may be in the pool at all ([06-resource-economics.md](06-resource-economics.md)). Byzantine members may equivocate, withhold, delay, collude, and time messages adversarially. The network is partially synchronous: safety never depends on timing; liveness resumes under eventual synchrony.

**Globally, an honest supermajority with bounded adaptivity.** Sharding changes the question from "is one committee honest" to "does *every* committee, drawn from a common pool, stay under f Byzantine". The defenses are statistical and procedural:

- **Unpredictable assignment.** Committees are sampled (`sample_committee`, pool draws) from beacon randomness — an accumulator re-mixed every epoch from the shard blocks' mandatory reveal leaves, folded over a consensus-derived range so that no party chooses which reveals count. Assignment for epoch e+1 is determined only at epoch e's fold, so an adversary cannot know placements far ahead. Why the seed resists steering and what its residual lever costs is §1.3; how the recoverable and terminal tiers are defended is §1.4. (A uniform `id % num_shards` layout exists only as the static genesis/test bootstrap constructor.)
- **Continuous churn.** Membership never sits still (§2), so corrupting a specific shard's committee is a moving target: by the time a slowly-adaptive adversary corrupts members of shard S, rotation has replaced some of them.
- **Expulsion.** Provable misbehavior removes the offending identity from its committee on evidence — permanently for self-proving equivocation (a double-signed beacon ballot, or a shard double-vote carried to the beacon as a witness leaf), for a cooldown for chronic liveness faults (§3). Removal, not confiscation: stake is never slashed.

The residual assumption — that random sampling from a <1/3-corrupt global pool yields <1/3-corrupt committees with overwhelming probability — is stated explicitly as INV-SEC-1 in the invariant register because it is an *assumption* the deployment must size for, not a theorem the code enforces. The claim this section defends is deliberately bounded: a sharded deployment that holds INV-SEC-1 is **security-competitive with an unsharded BFT chain while scaling far beyond one** — not safer than one. The per-committee less-than-one-third bound is the same primitive an unsharded chain rests on; what sharding adds is a conversion step between an adversary's share of the network and the share of any one committee it can actually reach, and that conversion rate (§1.2) — not the one-third line itself — is the system-level security parameter. The subsections below are its treatment: what the corruption tiers actually mean (§1.1), what the sampling converts and tolerates (§1.2), the randomness the draw rides on (§1.3), what recovers the recoverable failure and prevents the unrecoverable one (§1.4), and the edges the protocol cannot bound (§1.5).

### 1.1 Units, and the three corruption tiers

The corruption level everything below prices is the share of the pool's *seats* whose operators are adversarial — not the share of its stake (voting is per seat, above). The two coincide only when honest operators stake near the per-seat minimum: an honest operator staking several multiples of the minimum on one seat proportionally discounts the stake an adversary needs to reach a given seat share. Vnodes close that wedge — they push the marginal cost of a seat toward its stake, so rational operators split into many minimum-stake seats ([06-resource-economics.md](06-resource-economics.md)). That makes vnodes load-bearing for the security model, not just for hardware economics.

Committee corruption is not one condition but three tiers, and they could hardly differ more:

- **Below f+1 corrupt seats — guaranteed safety.** The classical BFT regime, and the only tier the word "safe" applies to: with fewer than a third of the committee corrupt, the safe-vote discipline and quorum intersection make agreement unconditional (INV-SHARD-1) — no timing assumptions, no probabilities, no recovery machinery involved. Per committee, this is exactly the primitive an unsharded BFT chain offers; everything else in this section exists to keep every committee in this tier.
- **f+1 through 2f corrupt seats — recoverable degradation, not guaranteed safety.** Here the BFT arguments lapse, and this band must never be called safe. Withholding votes halts the shard — other shards keep committing, this one stops — and the halt is detected and undone (§1.4). A fork is genuinely possible: quorum intersection forces every route to it through signed vote pairs no honest key can produce — self-proving equivocation, at its tightest a double-commit across two consecutive equivocated rounds — so the honest locks bias the attack toward the loud halt, but they do not preclude the double-commit; the double-signs are self-proving, and a replica that holds both revokes the offender's key (§3). The tier is recoverable — the re-draw and fence of §1.4 flush it — and its residual is a bounded cross-shard exposure window during recovery, closed forward by a network-wide fence (§1.4); what can leak inside it is chain *choice* between internally correct chains — honest co-signers still recompute every execution result — never a forged outcome.
- **2f+1 corrupt seats — terminal.** A committee at 2f+1 is unilateral control: it commits false state under a valid QC with no equivocation, so nothing self-proving ever exists, no detector fires (the committee stays live while it lies), and no in-protocol verifier re-executes a committee the design trusts. One captured shard can mint stake — the network-wide admission gate — and cascade everywhere. There is no recovery from this, and the design does not pretend one exists.

That asymmetry organizes the whole model: the guaranteed tier is the product being defended, the middle tier gets a **recovery**, the terminal tier gets a **prevention**, and the latter two are priced against different budgets.

### 1.2 Sampling: the conversion rate, and pool hygiene

What sharding changes is captured by one rate: how much *achievable single-shard control* a given share of the network converts into. An unsharded chain is the one-to-one baseline by definition — an adversary holding some fraction of the stake holds exactly that fraction of the only committee, no more and no less. Sampling leaves the *mean* precisely there: a given fraction of the pool is the same fraction of every committee in expectation. But the adversary does not attack the mean — it needs one committee over threshold, so it attacks the **maximum** over shards, a tail statistic. Three forces set that tail. Committee size concentrates each draw toward the mean, pushing the conversion toward one-to-one; small committees leave it high-variance. Shard count works against it — one lucky shard suffices, so the risk compounds across shards — the union-bound tax paid for scale. And assignment integrity decides whether the rate holds at all: a steerable or grindable draw lets the adversary *place* its seats instead of gambling them, collapsing the conversion until a small fraction of the network suffices for one whole shard — which is why the randomness of §1.3 is the linchpin of the competitive claim, not a supporting detail. One further difference from an unsharded chain is of kind, not degree, and is stated plainly: a single chain's safety is a hard threshold — intact below a third of stake, gone above — while sharded safety is probabilistic, a risk curve that rises smoothly with pool corruption. The design goal is to hold that curve competitive with the hard threshold while scaling; the spec prices the curve, this section states its shape.

A fresh committee is a random sample of the pool, and the trickle shuffle (§2) preserves that exactly: a committee that has been rotating one seat at a time for months is statistically indistinguishable from one freshly drawn this epoch. That is not a modelling convenience — the shipped fold is driven through hundreds of thousands of simulated shuffle events in CI and its step-by-step behavior matched against the ideal, and the spec's §7 walks each way reality deviates from the ideal (victim readiness, reshape pauses, pool depletion, corruption units coarser than a seat) with a measurement.

Committee size is capped by message complexity, and the cap inverts the sizing question. Growing committees eightfold buys only a few points of tolerable pool corruption, while the odds of a bad draw worsen ten-thousand-fold for every five points of pool corruption — a cliff. So the failure budget converts into a *pool-hygiene requirement*: at the analyzed operating point (128-seat committees, a failure budget that keeps a hundred-shard network's compound risk negligible over years — [committee_security.md](../specs/committee_security.md) §3, §6), the pool must stay under roughly 13% corrupt seats. Every mechanism that suppresses pool corruption — the market-clearing stake price, jailing, expulsion — buys more security per unit than seats do. Committee size is the dial; pool hygiene is the lever.

The tiers of §1.1 turn that one line into two operating bands:

- **Healthy — pool corruption below roughly 13%.** The middle tier stays out of reach: halts are vanishingly rare and every committee sits in the guaranteed tier with overwhelming probability.
- **Sound — below roughly 40%.** The terminal boundary is another matter: an unsteered draw concentrating 2f+1 corrupt seats in one committee is improbable enough that the same budget tolerates a pool approaching two-fifths corrupt seats. That is a risk bound on the unsteered draw, not a comparative claim — a steered draw voids it outright (§1.3), which is exactly why it bounds the terminal tier rather than advertising headroom.

Between the bands the network degrades without breaking: past roughly 13%, shards enter the middle tier with growing frequency — halts, re-draws, recovery windows carrying the exposure residual of §1.1; availability suffers, badly at the top of the band — but unilateral control never assembles. Sharding's security loss relative to a single chain is confined to an availability threshold that recovers.

### 1.3 Randomness integrity: protecting the draw itself

Everything in §1.2 prices an *unsteered* draw, and a steerable seed voids those prices wholesale. An adversary who can choose among candidate seeds each epoch — a menu that doubles with every contribution it can toggle — turns near-impossible odds into a directed march: steering each single-seat shuffle step to add a corrupt seat and never lose one, it reaches f+1 on a targeted shard in days. Committee size does not dilute this — flipping one folded input rehashes the entire seed — and honest entropy does not fix it, because the defect is *move order*: whoever reveals last, with full sight of everyone else's contribution, gets to pick from the menu.

The epoch seed therefore removes the last-mover position structurally. Every shard block carries a mandatory reveal leaf — a hash-VRF output fixed by the proposer's key and the block's slot, verified at block validation, appended to the per-shard beacon-witness accumulator — and the seed folds each crossing shard's watermark-to-boundary leaf range, the same quorum-certified chunk stream that already mutates beacon state ([01 §3.2](01-consensus-layers.md)). There is no include/omit toggle: the folded range is consensus-derived, and a block without its valid leaf is invalid — propose or forfeit the slot. Interior reveals are blind unconditionally: a leaf's certified position in the accumulator proves the later leaves in the fold did not yet exist when it was committed, a fact about chain structure rather than network timing. What remains is the window edge — the proposer closing a shard's last epoch window can pick among a handful of already-known candidate folds, or forfeit to a blind redraw by an unknown successor — a one-sided lever, available only while the adversary holds that closing slot, priced strictly below the fallback ceremony's grind at every shard count and far below anything that marches. An epoch where no chunk folds at all (bootstrap, or suppressing every shard's crossing at once — network-scale and self-announcing) falls back to the beacon committee's own reveal mix, the one place the classical last-revealer grind survives; the fallback is loud, never a quiet per-epoch option. The fold's window arithmetic is fork-critical — an off-by-one is a committee-selection fork, not a bookkeeping bug — and is model-checked through window, reshape, and recovery churn (`witness_fold.qnt`, [specs/README.md](../specs/README.md)).

### 1.4 Recovering f+1, preventing 2f+1

A middle-tier withholding failure *is* a detectable halt. The beacon already tracks each shard's committed-boundary watermark, so a shard quiet past a threshold — reshape-exempt, since reshaping shards are legitimately quiet — is flagged deterministically, and the recovery is a full re-draw of all n seats, never a trickle. The full draw is the grind-resistant move: assembling f+1 corrupt seats in one fresh draw is so improbable that even an adversary steering the seed barely improves its odds, whereas one-seat trickle steps are exactly what a grinder marches. The same detection fold revokes the old committee's cross-shard authority network-wide — a beacon-mandated fence, structured as a shard termination on the reshape machinery — because a beyond-f committee can still certify an orphan branch and export stale execution certificates during the window (the recovery-bridge finding, `shard_recovery.qnt`). The fence closes the leak forward; the residual is the detection-latency window, which makes the halt threshold a safety parameter, not merely a latency knob. A second failure shape is caught by different machinery than the halt trigger: a middle-tier committee can fork *actively* instead of halting — run two internally consistent heads and hand each to a different consumer audience — and, since it keeps crossing, the quiet-watermark trigger never flags it. The catch is on the consuming side. Cross-shard consumption requires a *commit proof* of the source block — the block's committing round-contiguous two-chain, not a bare QC (§5) — so a merely certified sibling is unconsumable, and an attacker cannot fool two victims with two certifications; to reach two audiences it must actually double-commit, and two committed chains at one height cannot exist below f+1 corrupt seats (INV-SHARD-1). At that point the two victims' commit proofs *are* the evidence: together they assemble a self-proving `ConflictingCommits`, which gossips network-wide, fences the shard on every honest node (mempool quiesce, proposer exclusion, provision abstention — a local quiesce, never a new validity verdict, so honest replicas never diverge on gossip timing), and funnels into the same full committee re-draw the halt path uses, under a fork recovery cause; where the round layout leaves a same-round double-sign, the equivocating seats are jailed, sound under registration proof of possession (INV-SEC-10). The boundary fold still only canonicalizes one crossing per shard per epoch (INV-BEACON-5 canonicalizes; it does not detect), so the fold carries no active-fork detector — detection lives on the consumer and gossip paths, where the fork is self-proving without any beacon cross-check. One residual is stated rather than papered over: a *perfect* suppressor that hand-delivers disjoint heads so no single node ever holds both branches evades the consumer-side catch by construction, and no shipped detector stands behind it — closing it would take a boundary-pinning comparison of each victim's committed head against the beacon-canonical boundary, deliberately out of scope. The suppression itself is fragile — committed headers gossip globally, so one replica holding both branches is enough to assemble the proof — and the accountability the re-draw imposes makes the attempt net-negative: the underlying double-signs are self-proving, so a replica holding both votes revokes the offender's key through the witness channel (§3), and honest co-signers recompute every result. The attacker economics collapse: days of grinding buy a bounded halt on one shard, then the foothold flushes and the march restarts from scratch. The trigger is outcome-based and asks nothing about intent — which is why it works where behavior-based penalties are blunted by honest-looking delay — and it rides beacon liveness (pool ratification), which the halted shard cannot touch.

For the terminal threshold there is nothing to trigger on, so the defense is holding the *steered* dynamics below a boundary the unsteered draw cannot reach (§1.2's roughly-40% line). The levers stack, all on the input side: the structurally grind-resistant seed (§1.3) is the deepest cut; behind it sit a deliberately small beacon committee (bounding how many seeds the fallback ceremony's grinder can choose among), FIFO seat eviction (fixed tenure denies the grind its victim-steering, so a targeted shard's corruption levels off at a ceiling instead of climbing), jail-on-first for withholding on the fallback path, and a recency-weighted resample that rate-limits how often any seat serves the beacon. Underneath all of them is pool hygiene: concentrating 2f+1 corrupt seats on one shard requires that many corrupt seats in play at all, so every point of pool corruption removed is worth more than any parameter. The stack is priced together in the spec's §10; its residuals — the fallback-path grind, the window-edge lever, the thin margins at high corruption — are stated there rather than hidden.

### 1.5 What the protocol cannot bound

Two edges are structural, and the model is honest about both. **Corruption units coarser than a seat**: the seat-count arithmetic does not care how corrupt seats are related — only how many there are — but the *cost* of corrupting them collapses when one unit controls many seats, since a compromised machine or a bribed controlling entity flips every seat it holds at once. The protocol cannot attribute seats to controlling entities — distinct stake-pool identities are indistinguishable from distinct operators, the same Sybil limit that makes stake rather than identity the admission gate — so no identity-keyed cap can bind, and spread across independent operators and infrastructure is an operational premise the protocol cannot verify. **No economic floor under bribery**: there is no slashing anywhere, deliberately (§3), so provable misbehavior burns the operator's key and franchise, never the capital — a re-registration tax, not a bribe floor. The defense against a bribed committee is therefore structural, not economic: below 2f+1, what such an adversary reliably obtains is a halt plus a fenced exposure window, and a fork additionally costs it self-proving double-signing that biases the attempt back toward the halt (§1.1) — recoverable degradation, not silent compromise; above 2f+1 only the prevention margins of §1.4 stand. Both edges point the same direction: the system's real bet is the sampling margins plus pool hygiene, which is exactly where the analysis puts the lever.

## 2. Shuffling

The beacon's fold rotates committee membership as a **trickle**: once per shuffle interval, each shard (skips aside) has one member rotated out and a replacement drawn from the pool. The interval is derived from genesis config rather than stored (`BeaconChainConfig::shuffle_interval_epochs`): `SHUFFLE_SYNC_HEADROOM · ready_timeout_epochs / shard_size`, floored at one epoch. That pins per-seat tenure at the headroom multiple of the sync budget whatever the committee size, and caps both the mid-sync fraction of a committee and each seat's sync duty cycle at the headroom's reciprocal — rotation runs at the fastest cadence the ready margin allows, and no faster, because adaptive-corruption resistance grows exponentially in rotation speed while its honest cost grows only linearly (spec §6 Rider 1).

Design points, each load-bearing:

- **Trickle, not reshuffle.** Wholesale re-assignment would be strongest against adaptive corruption but would force whole committees to re-sync state continuously. One-member-per-interval keeps every committee continuously above quorum with at most one syncing seat, while still fully turning committees over on a bounded timescale — liveness and adaptivity-resistance traded deliberately.
- **Tenure-fixed victim selection.** The longest-tenured *ready* member rotates out — a fixed clock, not a randomness draw, because a seeded victim hands a randomness grinder a lever to steer eviction away from corrupt seats every interval (§1.4); on a tenure clock every seat ages out on schedule. Ready members only: rotating out a not-yet-ready member would be a no-op for security and a real cost to liveness. The *entrant* stays a seeded pool draw — a deterministic entrant would make each shard's next placement predictable far ahead.
- **Ordering prevents self-replacement.** The victim is removed, the replacement drawn, and only then is the victim's status flipped to pooled — so it cannot be drawn straight back into the seat it left (it can legitimately land in a different shard later in the same step).
- **Reshape-aware skips.** Shards with pending splits carry their members to their children; merge keepers are pinned; rotating either would seat successor committees below quorum ([02-dynamic-sharding.md](02-dynamic-sharding.md) §5). Skips also apply when the pool is empty or no ready member exists — a rotation that shrinks a committee is worse than none.
- **Top-up.** A separate fold pass refills any committee below target size from the pool — the repair path for committees left short by reshape-depleted pools.

What the churn buys, exactly: against a *static* adversary the trickle is a minor statistical improvement over wholesale redraws (§1.2's guarantees do not depend on it); its real value is operational — at most one syncing seat per committee — and adaptive. The spec computes the adaptive campaign exactly rather than by heuristic, and the result is asymmetric: no plausible rotation speed flushes an adversary who can corrupt a seated validator in hours, while a corruption pipeline that takes days is forced onto steeply worsening odds as the interval shrinks. Rotation converts slow, cheap corruption into fast, expensive corruption; it does not stop fast corruption. Two sharper results bound the adversary further: a budget-limited attacker's best policy is to *conserve* — wait for lucky rotations to lift a committee near the boundary, then spend everything, which beats blind sustained pressure severalfold — and the interval itself is a two-way trade, since adaptive resistance wants it short while randomness-grind exposure wants it long (§1.3). The derivation resolves the trade on the adaptive side by construction — the ready margin is the only brake — and the spec prices the grind exposure at the derived cadence, where it is contained by the fallback path's crossing-suppression cost and the halt detector rather than by a slower clock (spec §10).

## 3. Jailing, witnesses, and accountability

Accountability acts only on **on-chain evidence**, and it is removal, not confiscation — there is no slashing anywhere. A jailed validator's stake stays in its pool and remains withdrawable ([06-resource-economics.md](06-resource-economics.md)); what is at risk is the seat and, for equivocation, the key.

The handoff from §2's rotation is itself quantified (spec §6 Rider 1): rotation degrades a corruption campaign in proportion to its length relative to a seat's tenure, and a campaign much shorter than a tenure — below roughly a tenth of it — passes rotation essentially untouched. Against compromise that fast, this section and the halt path of §1.4 are the whole defense: any fork must run through self-proving evidence, the vote lock biases the attack toward a halt, and the residual exposure is the middle tier's bounded recovery window (§1.1), not silent compromise.

The target is the operator, not the capital, by design. Pools may hold third-party delegated stake — delegator-level accounting lives in the shard-layer staking contract, and the beacon fold sees only the aggregate that sets a pool's activation count — and delegators cannot foresee an operator's first equivocation. Slashing would burn that innocent capital, and where an operator runs mostly delegated stake it would barely touch the party who actually signed; the consensus layer cannot even see the split to slash it fairly. Permanent key-revocation instead falls on the operator's franchise: the seats, and the on-chain reputation it must rebuild to attract delegation under a fresh identity. The principal stays whole and withdrawable; the decision-maker bears the loss. That is also why the security is structural rather than economic: a seat jailed on evidence is a single-use, non-steerable asset — able to regain relevance to any shard only through the beacon's random draw (§2), never by the operator's choice. The evidence pipeline is itself Byzantine-resistant:

- **Beacon-ballot equivocation → permanent key-revocation.** Two signed beacon prefix-consensus ballots at one position for different values are self-proving cryptographic proof the key is hostile — no correct node with a secure key can produce them. Beacon replicas that observe the pair ride it on the next proposal, the fold re-verifies both signatures, and the identity is jailed with no unjail path. The key is burned, but the operator's stake is untouched: they may withdraw it and re-register under a fresh identity, so permanence is a revoked key plus a re-registration cost, not a destroyed stake.
- **Shard-vote equivocation → permanent key-revocation, through the witness channel.** The safe-vote lock stops any honest key from producing two votes at one (height, round); a replica receiving such a pair detects it (vote tracking distinguishes it from legitimate revoting at a higher round after a timeout) and assembles the two signed votes into self-proving evidence. That evidence rides the shard's beacon-witness accumulator — the proposer carries it on the block, co-signers re-verify it as a block-validity condition so the QC attests it, and the beacon fold jails the identity permanently, stake untouched. Detection is best-effort: it needs one replica to hold both votes, so an equivocator that splits its votes across separate collectors escapes the jail — its fork still caught structurally by §1.1's quorum intersection.
- **Chronic liveness faults → cooldown jail.** Every skipped proposal round generates a `MissedProposal` witness leaf, deterministically, on every replica; the beacon aggregates per-validator counters and jails on threshold. Performance jails are recoverable: an `Unjail` witness after a cooldown, gated on pool capacity.
- **The witness channel cannot be censored selectively.** Witness leaves live in a per-shard merkle accumulator whose root is QC-attested in every block header. A proposer building block B must extend the accumulator with *all* leaves implied by the chain since the parent — including missed-proposal leaves for skipped rounds — because verifiers recompute the expected root over the same prospective walk (committed state plus uncommitted certified ancestors) and reject a header whose root omits any (INV-SEC-4). A proposer can therefore not quietly drop the leaf that would jail a friend, or mint one to jail a rival: chunks applied at the beacon are contiguous, merkle-proven, and watermarked exactly-once ([01-consensus-layers.md](01-consensus-layers.md) §3.3).
- **Jailing feeds back into topology safely.** A jailed validator leaves its committee immediately; the ready-filtered consensus view shrinks (so quorum math stays sound), and pool draws refill the seat.

## 4. Cryptographic layer

- **Scheme.** BLS12-381 throughout consensus. Aggregation is the enabler: a QC or EC is one aggregated signature plus a signer bitfield over the committee, verified by aggregating the (bitfield-selected) public keys — O(n) key aggregation, O(1) pairing, compact wire size independent of committee size.
- **Proof-of-possession at registration.** Aggregation's classical weakness is the rogue key: a key crafted against an honest one so the pair's aggregate collapses to a secret the attacker knows, letting a forged aggregate present the honest seat as a co-signer. Every post-genesis registration therefore carries the registrant's signature over its own key, bound to the network and claimed validator id, verified in the beacon fold before the key enters the registry (INV-SEC-10) — the rogue construction is unregisterable because its secret is unknowable. This is what licenses the verifiers to aggregate topology keys without per-use validation. Genesis keys are exempt as operator-trusted config.
- **Domain separation everywhere.** Every signed message type — block votes, timeouts, execution votes, PC/SPC ballots, ready signals — has its own domain tag, and messages bind their full context (network, shard, height, round, hashes). No signature can be replayed across contexts (INV-SEC-5).
- **Committee resolution is part of verification.** Verifying any certificate means resolving the committee from the artifact's canonical weighted timestamp via the topology schedule ([01-consensus-layers.md](01-consensus-layers.md) §4) and checking quorum against *that* membership. There is no ambient "current committee" — an artifact from any epoch in the retention window verifies against exactly the committee that governed it.
- **Post-quantum posture.** The signature layer is a swappable seam; a researched migration path exists (hash-based WOTS+ signatures over Poseidon2 with STARK aggregation) with benchmarked feasibility, and the JMT's generic hasher anticipates the same transition. Not deployed; noted here because the design deliberately avoids coupling that would block it.

## 5. What one shard trusts about another

The inter-shard trust surface is minimal and uniform: **quorums and proofs, never nodes.**

| Artifact | Trust basis |
|---|---|
| Remote chain progress | Remote block headers, QC-verified against the WT-resolved remote committee (remote-header sync, [03-state-and-sync.md](03-state-and-sync.md) §6) |
| Remote state values (provisions) | JMT multiproof into the remote header's QC-attested state root |
| Remote execution outcomes | ExecutionCertificates: 2f+1 aggregated signatures of the remote committee, outcome vector bound to the receipt root at decode |
| A terminated shard's final word | Terminal header + beacon-attested settled-waves root ([02-dynamic-sharding.md](02-dynamic-sharding.md) §4) |
| The topology itself | Each node's own fold of the beacon chain — never a peer's claim |

Two of these rows carry an extra gate at *consumption*, not just verification. A remote provision or execution certificate is consumed only against a **commit proof** of its source block — the block's committing round-contiguous two-chain, plus the parent-hash link down to a prefix-committed block, never a bare QC. A QC certifies availability, not canonicality: a middle-tier committee can certify two blocks at one height without breaking the safe-vote rule (INV-SHARD-1 forbids two *commits*, not two QCs), so consuming a bare certification would let it export from a branch that never commits. The commit proof closes that gap — the certified-but-uncommitted sibling is unconsumable — and forces any committee that means to serve two victims to double-commit, which is itself a self-proving fork (§1.4). It costs the consumer nothing: the source broadcasts provisions on commit and already holds the committing two-chain, so the proof rides the same header gossip one commit behind, well inside the wave deadline.

One seam is weaker than the rest and documented as such: provision `owned_nodes` claims are currently attested at transaction-hash granularity rather than proven into the QC-attested roots byte-for-byte — a Byzantine source has a bounded window to inject bogus ownership, with impact contained to liveness (deterministic aborts, declaration-bounded writes), not safety. See [04-atomic-commitment.md](04-atomic-commitment.md) §5 for the containment argument and the planned hardening.

## 6. Bounded resources: surviving adversarial input volumes

Safety rules make Byzantine *content* harmless; resource bounds make Byzantine *volume* harmless.

- **Per-(height, round) header caps.** At most a small constant number of distinct headers are buffered per slot (an honest proposer signs exactly one; excess is equivocation evidence), with eviction preferring rounds nearest the certified chain.
- **Bounded speculation.** View synchronization advances a replica's view only within a capped gap of its highest QC, and speculative verification of far-round blocks is capped — a flood of high-round garbage cannot inflate views or burn verification CPU.
- **Decode-time size caps.** Wire types use bounded collections (`BoundedVec`, `BoundedBytes`) that reject oversize claims *before allocation* — no peer-controlled length reaches an allocator (INV-SEC-6).
- **Message classes and shedding.** All traffic carries a `MessageClass` on a priority spectrum from live consensus down to bulk catch-up, and the sheddable tail — recovery and bulk transfers — is bounded: bounded queues, capped slot-pool shares, drop-on-overflow. Under load, catch-up and gossip traffic degrade; live consensus does not (INV-SEC-7). This ordering broke a real feedback loop in which a lagging node's bulk backfill starved the very consensus traffic that would have let it stop lagging. Gossip verdicts (accept/reject, feeding libp2p peer scoring) are emitted on every path, so misbehaving gossip peers lose reputation deterministically.
- **Backoff discipline.** All fetch paths (sync FSMs, settled-set acquisition, provision fallback) carry exponential backoff and peer rotation, so a dead or malicious serving peer converts to bounded retry load elsewhere, not a hammering loop.

## 7. Adversarial testing

The fault-injection layer (`Engine` rules on the simulated network; portable fault scenarios in `crates/scenarios`) exercises these defenses deterministically: targeted message-type drops (with probability, direction, and time-window selectors), asymmetric partitions, and crash/rejoin — all on a fault RNG stream seeded disjointly from the master seed, so fault schedules replay byte-identically ([07-determinism-and-testing.md](07-determinism-and-testing.md)). Standing assertions include: no committable fork exists under adversarial scheduling (the `fork_safety` harness asserts `find_fork().is_none()`), partitioned committees halt rather than diverge and resume on heal, isolated validators don't block quorum, and every cross-shard DA channel's loss is recovered by its fetch fallback.

## 8. Properties (summary)

Consolidated with IDs in [08-invariants.md](08-invariants.md). The verified models fix the Byzantine count at ≤ f per committee — INV-SEC-1 as an axiom — and §1 prices the chance that the axiom fails, so system-level claims compose by simple addition: the chance of a safety violation is at most the chance some committee ever exceeds its threshold, plus the chance of a violation while every committee stays within it (zero, for verified properties within their bounds).

- **INV-SEC-1 (committee honesty — assumption).** Every committee has <1/3 Byzantine members. Maintained statistically by seeded sampling + churn + expulsion from a <1/3-corrupt global pool; all consensus-layer safety invariants are conditional on it.
- **INV-SEC-2 (shuffle soundness).** Rotation is seeded-deterministic, one seat per shard per interval, never self-replacing, never touching mid-reshape shards, never shrinking a committee.
- **INV-SEC-3 (accountable expulsion).** Beacon-ballot and shard-vote equivocation are each self-proving and permanently revoke the key (stake is not slashed) — the shard-vote pair carried to the beacon on a QC-attested witness leaf; liveness-fault jailing derives only from merkle-proven, exactly-once-applied witness leaves and lifts after a cooldown.
- **INV-SEC-4 (witness completeness).** A block's witness root must commit every leaf implied by the chain — proposers can neither omit nor forge witness leaves.
- **INV-SEC-5 (signature hygiene).** All signatures are domain-separated and context-bound; all certificates verify against the WT-resolved committee for their artifact.
- **INV-SEC-6 (bounded decode).** No peer-supplied length is trusted before allocation; per-slot buffers are capped.
- **INV-SEC-7 (priority isolation).** Sheddable traffic classes cannot starve consensus-critical classes.
